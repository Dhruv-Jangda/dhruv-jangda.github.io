Volatility Index

Approach

Given OHLC data of S&P 500 for all 500 securities, following steps are taken to create its weekly volatility index:

  1. Separating ticker-wise data from the given data and arranging each ticker data in order of date.

  2. For each ticker data, calculating daily returns as:

    $return_{daily}=\frac{price_{close}}{price_{open}} - 1$

  3. Creating a table to store weekly information that will act as basis for index calculation.
  4. Getting last trading day of each week and number of trading days in the week across the period provided in the given data. Saving the same in the table created above.
  5. Getting other information for each day as saved in above table from given data as:
    • Close Price
    • Open Price
    • Weekly returns in same fashion as daily returns calculated above.

  6. Calculating weekly volatility with daily return for all trading days $t$ as:

    $volatility_{week}=\sigma(return_{daily})\times\sqrt{t}$

  7. Calculating variable weekly volatility with weekly return till the last weekday as:

    $\overline{volatility_{week}}=volatility_{week}\times\sigma(return_{week})$

    Note – The steps 3 and 4 are followed for every ticker

  8. Creating tables to store most and least volatile stocks for a week.
  9. For each last trading date of week:
    • Collecting all information as gathered in above steps for each ticker.
    • Arranging in ascending order of weekly volatility and saving data of top 10 securities to the table for the least volatile stocks.
    • Arranging in descending order of weekly volatility and saving data of top 10 securities to the table for the least volatile stocks.
    • Updating both tables for each last trading date.
  10. Saving the results to project directory.
Observations
  1. Most Volatile
    • Securities that occurred more than 50 times are : CHK, INCY, AMD, FCX
    • There are 95 Securities that appeared just once.
  2. Least Volatile
    • Securities that occurred more than 20, but less than 30 times : PG, PEP, MON, TMK, KO
    • here are 64 Securities that appeared just once.
  3. There are 334 securities that were present in both lists.
  4. There are 110 securities that appeared less than 10 times in the list of most volatile and were almost equally in the other list.
Conclusion
  1. The 110 securities as in #2 due to being there very few times in both the lists may be low risk investment options, yet providing returns and can be thought of long term investments.
  2. The 334 securities as in #2 may appear as an investment option that pose a balance between risk and returns.
  3. Other technical indicators can also be incorporated to make a better form of index.

Pair Trading

Approach

Given price data of S&P 500 for all 500 securities, following steps are taken to create pair trading strategy:

  1. Separating ticker-wise data from the given data and arranging each ticker data in order of date.

  2. For each year:

    • Create two square matrices of size $n_{tickers}\times n_{tickers}$ , one for storing variance values and the other for storing correlation values for tickers with respect to one another.

    • For each ticker:

      • Set base data as first ticker data for that year.
      • Traverse next ticker till last ticker.
      • Set current data as current ticker data.
      • Select data at common dates of both ticker data.

      • Assuming list of close prices $X$ and $Y$, with corresponding elements as $x$ and $y$, calculate:

        • Variation in difference of close prices of both tickers:

          $\sqrt{\frac{1}{n}\times\sum_{i=1}^n((x-y)_i - (\overline{X-Y})^2}$

          where $X$ is base price and $Y$ is current price

        • Correlation coefficient of close prices of both tickers:

          $\frac{\sum_{i=1}^n(x_i-\overline{X})\times(y_i-\overline{Y})}{\sqrt{[\sum_{i=1}^n(x_i-\overline{X})^2]\times[\sum_{i=1}^n(y_i-\overline{Y})^2]}}$

      • Store the calculated values in the corresponding matrices.

      • Calculate final score as element-wise product of both matrices.

        Note – In order to avoid repetition in calculation, the matrices will be of triangular(upper or lower) form

  3. Save the matrices for each year.
  4. Create tables to store top and bottom scores for each year.
  5. For each year:
    • Get corresponding matrix for the final score.
    • Arrange the scores in ascending order for bottom scores and descending for top scores.
    • For each score after ordering, locate the index in the matrix to know the ticker pair for the score.
    • Using the index, get variance and correlation values from corresponding matrices for both the orders.
    • Store the values(year, tickers, variance, correlation, similarity score) in the corresponding table for each order.
Observations
  1. All the pairs were different as most and least varying throughout the period 2013-2018.
  2. Pair A-AMZN was among least varying in years 2014, 2016 and 2017 but was the most varying in the year 2015.
  3. Year 2017 topped the list of most varying pairs, with CMG varying the most with BLK, BIIB and BK.
  4. A was present in the most varying list for years 2014-2017 at most twice with AZO, AMZN and AGN.
  5. Pair A-AZO was present in most varying pairs during years 2014, 2015 and 2017.

Quantitative Classifier

Approach

Given a ticker, its price history data, following steps are taken to create a classifier that predicts if the close price for a given date is either higher or lower than the open of the day:

  1. Feature Engineering:

    • Known factors from given data – Date, Volume, Open, High, Low and Close.

    • Using OHLC data, factors that emphasis analysis on volume movement, trend movement, its strength, price movement and price levels can be calculated by taking $O$ as $price_{open}$, $H$ as $price_{high}$, $L$ as $price_{low}$ and $C$ as $price_{close}$.

      • Candle Stick factors:

        $size = C - O$

        $shadow_{upper} = H - C$

        $shadow_{lower} = O - L$

        However, these factors are kept positive but with change of sign the reference will change. That’s why they are calculated here as above

      • Price Boundary factors:

        • Pivot

          $pivot=\frac{O+H+L+C}{4}$

        • Support Levels

          $support_1 = 2\times pivot-H$

          $support_2 = pivot-(H-L)

          $support_3 = price_-2\times(H-pivot)$

        • Resistance Levels

          $resistance_1 = 2\times pivot-L$

          $resistance_2 = pivot+(H-L)$

          $resistance_3 = H+2\times(pivot-L)$

      • Trend Indicators:

        • RSI - Relative Strength Index for $d$ days in a period:

          $\triangle_{price} = C-O$

          Strength($\gamma$) for period $p$: $\gamma_p = -1\times \frac{\sum_{i=1}^d\triangle_{price}(+)}{\sum_{i=1}^d\triangle_{price}(-)}$

          $RSI = 100 - \frac{100}{1+\gamma_p}$

        • DI - Directional Indicator

          $move_{up} = H_{next} - H_{current}$

          $move_{down} = L_{current} - L_{next}$

          • when $move_{up} > move_{down}$ and $move_{up} > 0$ => $DM(+) = move_{up}$

          • when $move_{up} < move_{down}$ and $move_{down} > 0$ => $DM(-) = move_{down}$

            True Range: $TR = \max[(H-L),(H-C),(L-C)]$

          • Smoothening($\sigma$) the factors $TR$, $DM(+)$ and $DM(-)$ across $d$ days in a period and $p$ days in the last period for a factor $f$ to form $ATR$, $smooth(+DM)$ and $smooth(-DM)$ where:

            Smoothen factor $f$ for a period of $d$ days: $\sigma(f,d)=\frac{\sigma(f_{previous})+(d\times f_{current})}{d}$

            First value of $\sigma$: $\sigma_1=\overline{f}_{p}$

            $\pm DI=100\times\frac{\sigma(\pm DM)}{ATR}$

            where $f_{previous}$ and $f_{current}$ are the values of factors at previous period and current period respectively.

        • ADX - Average Directional Index for period of $d$ days

          $DX=100\times \frac{\mid DI(+)-DI(-)\mid}{DI(+)+DI(-)}$

          $ADX=\sigma(DX,d)$

      • Average Indicators:

        • EMA - Exponential Moving Average for period of $d$ days:

          Weight: $w =\frac{2}{d+1}$

          First value of EMA: $EMA_1=\overline{C_d}$

          $EMA_i=(w\times C) + (1-w)\times EMA_{i-1}$

        • EMA Convergence-Divergence depicting mid and short for periods where days($d$) $d_mid > d_short$ : $EMA_{mid}-EMA_{short}$

      • Volume Indicators

        • OBV - On Balance Volume, for $V$ as trading volume taken for the day for $C_{current}$ or $C_{prev}$ whichever is maximum.

          $OBV_1=C$

          $OBV_i=OBV_{i-1}\pm V$

        • CVI - Chaikin Volume Indicator for period of $d$ days:

          Money Flow Multiplier: $MFM=\frac{(C-L)-(H-C)}{H-L}$

          Money Flow Volume: $MFV=MFM\times V$

          $CVI_d=\frac{\sum_{i=1}^dMFV}{\sum_{i=1}^dV}$

          Note - Calculations have been carried out on span of short-term of 4 days and mid-term of 14 days.

  2. Splitiing the dateset to Train and Test dataset.
  3. Preparing parameter grid for Grid Search Cross Validation.
  4. Using different estimators or multiple estimators using ensembled learning, performing the Grid Search CV.
  5. Evaluating the performance of best estimator on Train and Test data using metrics:
    • Precision
    • Recall
  6. Tuning the hyperparameters unless expected performance of the model.
  7. Saving the model for future use.

Conclusion
  1. Feature engineering always require an exhaustive research or a domain expertise and is a challenge in every machine learning pipeline.
  2. For every test date it is required to make the model again, since it saves the model from :
    • Look-Ahead bias - A model trained till the test date is used for prediction on a new test date prior to the previous one.
    • Underfitting - A model trained till the test date is used for prediction on a new test date later to the previous one.
